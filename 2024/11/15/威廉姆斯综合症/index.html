1 Introduction Williams syndrome [WS] is a genetic disorder associated with learning difficulties, with an incidence of 1 in 25,000 (Greenberg, 1990). It is caused by a microdeletion on the long arm of chromosome 7 at 7q11.23, which affects one allele of the elastin gene and other contiguous genes (Ewart et al., 1993; Tassabehji et al., 1996; Frangiskakis et al., 1996). Within cognitive skills, there is a spatial disorder, for example in drawing and relative strength in language. The development of language is also uneven but there is dispute about the actual performance on language tasks and the best theoreticalinterpretation of this performance.

In two previous studies investigating morpho-syntactic skills in subjects with WS, Clahsen and Almazan (1998, 2001) found that the subjects with WS do not show any syntactic impairment, and that they perform excellently on regular past tense and regular plural inflection. On the other hand, their scores on irregular past tense and irregular plural forms were found to be lower than those of unimpaired mental age controls. Moreover, the subjects with WS were seen to use regular rules of inflection excessively, even in circumstances in which unimpaired children (and adults) would not use them. Clahsen and Almazan proposed an account of this unusual pattern of morpho-syntactic skills, in terms of a modular view of the language faculty in which the knowledge of language is assumed to consist of two separate components, a lexicon of stored entries and a computational system of combinatorial operations to form larger linguistic expressions (e.g. Chomsky 1995, Pinker 1999). They argued that in WS, these two core modules of language are dissociated such that the computational (rule-based) system for language is selectively spared, while lexicalrepresentations and/or their access procedures are impaired.

Recently, however, this account of WS has been challenged, and an alternative non-modular connectionist model has been proposed to explain the performance of subjects with WS (Thomas & Karmiloff-Smith 1999, Thomas et al. 1999). According to this neuro-constructivist approach, the linguistic performance of a subject with WS is argued to reflect shifted constraints in the representation and development of language, whereby there is more weight on phonological information and less on semantic information. More specifically, subjects with WS are claimed to have ‘overly rich phonological representations’ which prevent them from learning the past tense

威廉姆斯综合征 [WS] 是一种与学习困难相关的遗传性疾病，其发生率为二十五万分之一（Greenberg, 1990）。该病由7号染色体长臂上的7q11.23微缺失引起，影响了一个弹力蛋白基因等位基因及其他相邻基因（Ewart et al., 1993; Tassabehji et al., 1996; Frangiskakis et al., 1996）。在认知技能方面，威廉姆斯综合征患者具有空间处理障碍，例如在绘图方面，而在语言方面则表现相对较强。尽管语言发展也不均衡，但对其在语言任务中的实际表现及其最佳理论解释仍存在争议。

在之前的两项研究中，Clahsen 和 Almazan (1998, 2001) 发现，WS 患者没有表现出任何句法障碍，并且他们在规则过去时和规则复数词形变化上表现优异。然而，他们在不规则过去时和不规则复数形式上的得分低于正常智力年龄对照组。此外，WS 患者在那些正常儿童（及成人）不会使用规则的情况下，过度使用了规则变化。Clahsen 和 Almazan 提出了一种解释这种非常规的形态句法能力模式的理论，即从模块化的语言功能观点出发，假设语言知识由两个独立的部分组成：存储词汇表和用于形成更复杂的语言表达式的组合运算系统（如 Chomsky, 1995; Pinker, 1999）。他们认为，在 WS 患者中，这两个核心的语言模块是分离的，使得基于规则的语言计算系统选择性地保留完好，而词汇表示及其访问程序则受损。

然而，近来对这一关于 WS 的解释提出了挑战，并提出了一种替代性的非模块化联结主义模型来解释 WS 患者的语言表现（Thomas & Karmiloff-Smith 1999, Thomas et al. 1999）。根据这种神经构建主义方法，WS 患者的语言表现反映了语言表征和发展过程中约束条件的转移，其中语音信息权重较大，语义信息权重较小。具体而言，据称 WS 患者具有“过于丰富的语音表征”，这妨碍了他们正确学习过去时态并将其推广到新项目。本研究批评了 Thomas 和 Karmiloff-Smith (1999) 以及 Thomas 等人 (1999) 的提议，并提供了来自三个来源的证据，针对 WS 患者的语言能力对此争议进行了探讨。将展示结果包括 WS 患者对比较级形容词的使用、接受性和产生性词汇量以及他们的阅读和写作技能。本文认为，这些新的发现进一步支持了 WS 中选择性词汇缺陷的假设，该假设不需要联结主义的解释，并且具有任务特异性，这对这类解释构成了挑战。

2 Impaired phonological representations in WS? Thomas and Karmiloff-Smith (Thomas and Karmiloff-Smith 1999, Thomas et al. 1999) argue that subjects with WS differ from unimpaired children and adults in their phonological representation system. The idea is that the language of subjects with WS develops under a different set of constraints from normal child language development and that subjects with WS place ‘more weight on phonological information and less weight on semantic information’ (Thomas et al. 1999: 40). This idea has been implemented in a connectionist network for past-tense formation in which the phonological input representations are overspecified in a way such that the network is unable to detect similarities between different phonological representations. In other words, phonological representations in subjects with WS are said to be ‘overly-detailed’ and more specific than those of unimpaired children. Thomas et al. (1999) argue that this difference explains why WS children perform worse on irregular past-tense formation than unimpaired controls; this is presumably because the stem and past-tense forms of irregular verbs tend to be phonologically more different from each other than those of regular verbs (compare teach – taught vs. walk – walked). Overly detailed phonological representations are also said to explain why the WS group produced significantly fewer irregularizations, i.e. overapplications of irregular past-tense forms, than typically developing children (p.30); in Thomas & Karmiloff-Smith’s account this is because the overspecified phonological representations of existing past-tense forms in WS prevent associative generalizations to new items. They stress that ‘the apparent deficit in irregular past tense formation

The discussion here will focus on their hypotheses ofdelayed language development and a phonological deficit in WS.

This is evident from their Fig.4, which shows that both in terms of chronological and verbal mental age, the older subjects with WS produce more –ed overregularizations to nonce words that rhyme with existing irregular verbs than the younger ones. The opposite pattern is found from the unimpaired controls: the older they get, the less often they add –ed to rhyming irregulars. This difference is hard to reconcile with the view that the linguistic profile of subjects with WS simply represents adelay of normal language development.

Another challenge for the delay hypothesis comes from the results of a recent study of plural formation and compounding (Clahsen & Almazan 2001). Results from three experiments showed that the subjects with WS excessively overapplied the regular plural –s, even in circumstances in which typically developing children hardly ever use –s plurals, namely as non-head elements inside compounds. In contrast to unimpaired controls, the subjects with WS maintained regular -s plurals inside compounds, producing ungrammatical derived compounds such as *rats-eater as well as many ungrammatical root compounds with regular -s plurals as non-head elements, *foots cake, *gooses fax, etc. In an additional comprehension experiment, the children with WS were found frequently to misinterpret compounds like red rats eater as the eater being red rather than the victims. This indicated that in contrast to unimpaired children, the presence of a regular plural did not have any effect on the interpretation of the compound by children with WS. The results from these three experiments show that in subjects with WS regular plurals can occur freely inside compounds. This is different from adult English as well as from early child language.
